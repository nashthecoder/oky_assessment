Reflections

What three days with the Oky codebase revealed — gaps, observations, and the questions I'd want answered before starting.

Advisory perspective TOR §3 & §4 Kilifi, Kenya

🔍 What the programme actually needs

The technical work in this assessment was straightforward — clone, run, fix, build. What was harder to ignore was everything sitting just beneath it. The app is polished. The CMS is invisible. And in a product that serves girls aged 10 and above across 15 countries, the content pipeline is at least as important as the codebase.

📂 The CMS has no documentation

There is no user guide, no editorial workflow, and no content style guide for the CMS. Country partners and local editors are expected to manage content — create articles, set targeting, publish — with no written reference. During this assessment a content filter field (Urban only / Rural only) was found to be entirely non-functional from the CMS side: the dropdown was empty, the value was never submitted, and no editor would have known the feature existed. It had been invisible since it was built.

💡 A CMS documentation audit — covering how to create, edit, and publish content; how targeting and age restrictions work; what the live toggle does and when to use it — is directly within scope under TOR §3 and §4. This is not new policy. It is making existing practice visible, durable, and transferable across staff turnover and 15 country deployments.
⚠️ Silent bugs are an editorial problem, not just a technical one

Two bugs found during Task 2 directly affect content editors — not developers. The live toggle silently saves articles as unpublished regardless of editor input. The delete button silently fails. In both cases the UI gives no feedback. An editor in Nairobi or Jakarta has no way to know their content isn't reaching users. These are not edge cases waiting to be reported — they are happening in production, on every Oky deployment, right now.

🌍 Developer onboarding is broken for contributors outside the core team

Five blockers hit in first-time setup — none documented anywhere. The README assumes Docker, which fails on older machines common in low-resource settings. The Expo SDK version requirement is undocumented. The workspace name is wrong in every how-to that exists. A new contributor in a partner country, trying to localise or adapt the app, would give up before getting it running. For a Digital Public Good, this is a DPG compliance gap, not just a convenience issue.

🙋🏾‍♀️ What I bring to this work

A few things about my context that felt relevant as I worked through this assessment — not as claims, but as honest observations about where I come from and how I approach things.

Context I am based in Kilifi, Kenya. The communities Oky serves are communities I work alongside through Mama Tech Africa — so the question of whether content reaches a girl in a rural county is not abstract to me.
Technical comfort Within this assessment I was able to navigate the CMS, EJS templates, TypeScript, and the database — enough to find and fix things, and enough to read a PR diff and give feedback a developer can act on.
Content lens Fixing the urban/rural filter mattered to me not just because it was broken code, but because a rural girl seeing the same content as an urban girl is a missed opportunity — for relevance and for trust. That framing shaped how I approached Task 2.
Documentation approach The proposals in this assessment — CONTRIBUTING, SECURITY, SETUP_LOG, README — were written with staff turnover in mind. Documentation that only makes sense to the person who wrote it isn't really documentation.
On using AI tools Claude Code was used throughout as a technical partner — for troubleshooting, documentation, and building these pages. I've been transparent about that throughout. It felt important to show how the role would actually be approached, not just what the output looks like.

❓ Questions I would want answered before starting

These are not doubts — they are the assumptions I have made that need testing. Getting them wrong early wastes everyone's time.

On the CMS and content

  1. 1Who currently owns the CMS in each country deployment — is there a designated content editor per country, or does the DCOE team manage content centrally?
  2. 2What is the current process for a country partner to request a new article, category, or translation — is there a workflow, or is it ad hoc?
  3. 3Has the urban/rural content filter been intentionally unused across deployments, or was it simply undiscovered? Are there country teams who have been asking for this kind of targeting?
  4. 4Is there a health or clinical review process before articles go live — and if so, who owns it and where is it documented?
  5. 5What is the reading-age standard for content? Is there a tone guide? The app serves girls from age 10 — has this been formally documented for content editors?

On the role and the team

  1. 1What does "technical advisory" look like in practice — embedded with partners, remote support, or both? What does a typical week look like in this role?
  2. 2What is the relationship between the DCOE tech team and country teams — are countries autonomous in their deployments, or centrally guided on technical decisions?
  3. 3Is documentation within scope to propose and publish directly, or does it go through an approval process? Who has the authority to merge changes to the Oky repo?
  4. 4Of the 15 countries, how many have active, maintained deployments right now? And how many have dedicated local technical contacts?

On the product direction

  1. 1Is there a product roadmap for the CMS, or is it considered stable and feature-complete? I ask because the urban/rural targeting suggests the architecture anticipates more sophisticated content targeting than is currently in use.
  2. 2What is the team's current thinking on low-bandwidth environments — is offline content access or cached encyclopedia data on the roadmap?
  3. 3Are there known gaps in the app for girls with disabilities or low literacy? Has this been assessed, and is it within scope for this role to raise?
